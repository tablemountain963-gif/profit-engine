## Overview
The UK Met Office's initiative to invite the public to suggest storm names has led to a significant response, with some people using the opportunity to raise awareness about climate change and the oil industry. The suggestion of "Storm Bigoil" and other similar names is seen as a way to highlight the impact of human activities on the environment. This approach is reminiscent of the 2016 incident where a £200 million polar research ship was almost named "Boaty McBoatface" following a public poll.

## Why It Matters
The naming of storms is not just a matter of assigning a label to a weather event; it also serves as a way to raise awareness about the potential dangers of severe weather. By engaging the public in the naming process, the UK Met Office aims to increase awareness and preparedness for storms. The trend of suggesting names like "Storm Bigoil" indicates a growing interest in environmental issues and a desire to hold companies accountable for their impact on the planet. For instance, in 2020, the UK experienced a series of severe storms that caused widespread damage and disruption, highlighting the need for effective storm naming and communication.

## How to Start
To get involved in the storm naming process, individuals can submit their suggestions to the UK Met Office through their website or social media channels. The Met Office typically accepts suggestions for storm names during the summer months, with the selected names being announced in the fall. It's essential to follow the guidelines set by the Met Office, which include suggesting names that are easy to pronounce, remember, and spell. Additionally, names should not be offensive or insensitive, and should not be too similar to existing storm names.

## Common Pitfalls
One potential pitfall of the public engagement approach is the risk of names being suggested that are not suitable for use in a professional or international context. For example, names that are too long, too complicated, or too similar to existing names may not be effective in communication. Furthermore, the use of names that are perceived as provocative or political may undermine the credibility of the storm naming process. To mitigate this risk, the Met Office has established a set of criteria for selecting storm names, which includes considering the potential impact on international communication and cooperation.
