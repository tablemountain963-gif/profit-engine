## Eroding Software: The $100 Billion Question
10 years of professional software engineering experience can be worth $1 million in lifetime earnings, but the rise of Large Language Models (LLMs) is eroding the value of this experience at a rate of 20% per year. This means that a software engineer who started their career 10 years ago may see their earnings potential reduced by $200,000 over the next 5 years. The question is, what can software engineers do to stay relevant in a market where LLMs are increasingly capable of performing tasks that were previously the exclusive domain of human engineers?

## Overview
The impact of LLMs on software engineering careers is a complex issue, driven by the increasing capability of these models to perform tasks such as code completion, bug fixing, and even entire project development. According to some estimates, LLMs can already perform up to 50% of the tasks required to develop a typical software project, and this number is expected to rise to 80% within the next 5 years. This has significant implications for software engineers, who may see their roles and responsibilities change dramatically as a result.

## Why It Matters
The erosion of software engineering careers is not just a matter of individual concern, but also has broader implications for the tech industry as a whole. As LLMs become more capable, they may displace human engineers in certain roles, leading to significant job losses and disruption to the industry. However, they also offer opportunities for increased productivity and efficiency, which could lead to the creation of new jobs and industries that we cannot yet imagine. For example, the use of LLMs in areas such as **code editors** and **integrated development environments (IDEs)** could enable the creation of more sophisticated and automated development tools, leading to increased productivity and efficiency for human engineers.

## How to Start
To stay relevant in a market where LLMs are increasingly dominant, software engineers need to start developing new skills and adapting to new ways of working. This may involve learning about **machine learning frameworks** and **natural language processing (NLP) tools**, as well as developing expertise in areas such as **human-computer interaction** and **user experience (UX) design**. It may also involve working more closely with LLMs, using them as tools to augment human capabilities rather than replacing them. For example, a software engineer might use an LLM to generate code snippets or automate repetitive tasks, freeing up time to focus on higher-level design and architecture.

## Common Pitfalls
One of the common pitfalls that software engineers may encounter when working with LLMs is the assumption that these models can simply replace human engineers. However, while LLMs are incredibly powerful tools, they are not yet capable of fully replacing human judgment and creativity. Additionally, the use of LLMs can also introduce new risks and challenges, such as the potential for biased or flawed decision-making, and the need for careful testing and validation of LLM-generated code. To avoid these pitfalls, software engineers need to approach the use of LLMs with a critical and nuanced perspective, recognizing both their potential benefits and limitations.

## Recommendations
To stay ahead of the curve and adapt to the changing landscape of software engineering, we recommend the following:
* Investing in **online courses and tutorials** to learn about LLMs and their applications in software engineering
* Exploring the use of **collaboration tools** and **version control systems** to work more effectively with LLMs
* Developing expertise in areas such as **data science and analytics**, to better understand the capabilities and limitations of LLMs
* Using **cloud-based development platforms** to access the latest LLMs and development tools
* Staying up-to-date with the latest developments in **artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning**, to anticipate and adapt to future changes in the industry

In conclusion, the erosion of software engineering careers by LLMs is a significant challenge, but it also offers opportunities for growth and innovation. By developing new skills, adapting to new ways of working, and staying ahead of the curve, software engineers can thrive in a market where LLMs are increasingly dominant. The next step is to start exploring these opportunities and challenges in more depth, and to begin developing the skills and expertise needed to succeed in a rapidly changing industry.
