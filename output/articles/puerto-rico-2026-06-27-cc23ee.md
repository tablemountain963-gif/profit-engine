$74 billion: that's the estimated debt Puerto Rico faced in 2016, prompting then-President Barack Obama to sign the PROMESA Law. The promise was to get the island's finances under control, but 10 years on, the reality is starkly different. Instead of fiscal stability, Puerto Rico has been subjected to austerity measures that have far-reaching consequences for its citizens.

## Overview
The PROMESA Law, or Puerto Rico Oversight, Management, and Economic Stability Act, was enacted to address the island's crippling debt. However, critics argue that the law has done more harm than good, imposing strict austerity measures that have limited the government's ability to provide essential services. The oversight board, established by the law, has been criticized for its handling of the situation, with many feeling that it has prioritized the interests of creditors over those of the Puerto Rican people.

## Why It Matters
The situation in Puerto Rico is a complex one, with far-reaching implications for the island's economy, healthcare system, and education sector. The austerity measures imposed by the PROMESA Law have led to significant cuts in funding for essential services, including healthcare and education. This has resulted in a brain drain, as many young and talented Puerto Ricans are forced to leave the island in search of better opportunities. Furthermore, the island's infrastructure is in dire need of repair, with many roads, bridges, and buildings still damaged from Hurricane Maria, which devastated the island in 2017.

## How to Start
For those looking to understand the situation in Puerto Rico, it's essential to start by examining the historical context that led to the PROMESA Law. This includes the island's complex relationship with the United States, as well as the economic factors that contributed to its debt crisis. It's also crucial to look at the impact of the law on the island's economy, healthcare system, and education sector. By understanding these factors, individuals can begin to appreciate the complexities of the situation and the need for a comprehensive solution.

## Common Pitfalls
One of the most significant pitfalls in addressing the situation in Puerto Rico is the tendency to oversimplify the issues. The island's debt crisis is a complex problem that requires a nuanced approach, taking into account the historical, economic, and social factors that have contributed to it. Another common pitfall is the failure to prioritize the needs of the Puerto Rican people, with many solutions focusing on the interests of creditors rather than the island's citizens.
