Chapter 1: Read This First

The most common misconception about Sneak Peak is that it's just a fancy term for a soft launch or a beta test. However, Sneak Peak is a specific strategy that involves releasing a limited version of your product or service to a small, targeted audience, with the goal of gathering feedback and refining your offering before a full-scale launch. For example, when Dropbox launched its Sneak Peak in 2008, it allowed just 2,000 users to test its service, resulting in a waitlist of 75,000 people. This approach helped Dropbox refine its product and create a buzz around its launch.

To illustrate the importance of Sneak Peak, consider the case of Gmail. When Gmail launched in 2004, it was initially available only to a limited number of users, with a strict invite-only policy. This allowed Google to test its product, gather feedback, and make improvements before opening it up to the general public. Gmail's Sneak Peak lasted for 18 months, during which time it refined its product and built a loyal user base. By the time Gmail was officially launched, it had already gained a significant following and was able to scale quickly.

A key aspect of Sneak Peak is its focus on feedback and iteration. Rather than trying to launch a perfect product, Sneak Peak encourages you to release a minimum viable product (MVP) and then refine it based on user feedback. This approach is often referred to as the "lean startup" methodology, popularized by Eric Ries in his book "The Lean Startup". By embracing this approach, you can reduce the risk of launching a product that doesn't meet the needs of your target market.
