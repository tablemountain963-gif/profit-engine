Chapter 1: Read This First

The one thing most people get wrong about review is that it's a one-time event, a box to be checked at the end of a project. In reality, effective review is an ongoing process that should start from the very beginning of a project. For example, using the Scrum framework, teams should dedicate 5-10% of their sprint time to review and retrospectives. This allows for continuous improvement and adaptation, rather than trying to cram all review into a single, often rushed, session at the end.

A concrete example of this is GitHub's code review process, where developers are expected to submit their code for review before it's merged into the main branch. This approach ensures that at least two people have looked at the code, reducing the likelihood of errors and improving overall quality. By incorporating review into the development process, teams can catch issues early, reducing the overall cost of fixing them by up to 70% according to a study by IBM.

To illustrate the importance of early review, consider a project where the development team spent 3 months building a feature, only to discover during the final review that it didn't meet the customer's requirements. If review had been incorporated earlier, this issue could have been caught and addressed much sooner, saving the team 2 months of rework. This is why it's essential to prioritize review from the outset, using tools like Trello or Asana to track progress and ensure that review is a continuous part of the process.

By understanding that review is not a one-time event, but rather an ongoing process, teams can set themselves up for success and avoid common pitfalls. Chapter 2: Establishing a Review Process

Establishing a review process involves several key steps, including defining the scope of review, identifying the right reviewers, and establishing clear criteria for evaluation. Using the Six Thinking Hats approach, teams can ensure that all aspects of a project are considered, from the creative to the critical. For example, a team might use Google Docs to create a shared document outlining the review process, including the scope, criteria, and roles and responsibilities.

A key part of establishing a review process is defining the scope of review. This involves identifying what will be reviewed, how often, and by whom. Using the Pareto principle, teams can focus on the 20% of the project that will have the greatest impact, rather than trying to review everything. For instance, a team might decide to review only the most critical components of a project, such as the user interface or the database schema.

To illustrate the importance of clear criteria, consider a project where the review team was unclear about what constituted a "passing" review. As a result, the team spent hours debating and re-reviewing the same issues, delaying the project by several days. By establishing clear criteria upfront, teams can avoid this kind of confusion and ensure that review is efficient and effective.
